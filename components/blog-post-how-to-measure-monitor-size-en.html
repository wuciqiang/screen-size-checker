How to Measure Monitor Size: 4 Easy Methods (2025 Guide)

Knowing your monitor size is essential when buying a new display, setting up a dual-monitor workspace, or troubleshooting compatibility issues. While it might seem straightforward, measuring monitor size correctly requires understanding what manufacturers mean by "screen size" and how to measure it properly.

Quick Answer: To measure monitor size, use a ruler or tape measure to measure the diagonal distance from one corner of the screen to the opposite corner, excluding the bezel (frame around the screen). This gives you the screen size in inches, which is how manufacturers specify monitor sizes (e.g., 24", 27", 32").

In this comprehensive guide, we'll show you 4 different methods to determine your monitor size, from physical measurement to digital tools, ensuring you get accurate results every time.

Why Monitor Size Measurement Matters

Understanding your monitor size is crucial for several reasons:

For Upgrades and Purchases:

  • Ensuring compatibility with monitor arms and stands
  • Comparing screen real estate between monitors
  • Planning desk space and ergonomic setup

For Technical Support:

  • Providing accurate specifications when seeking help
  • Verifying warranty coverage and model information
  • Troubleshooting resolution and scaling issues

For Workspace Planning:

  • Calculating optimal viewing distance
  • Designing multi-monitor setups
  • Ensuring proper desk dimensions

Understanding Monitor Size Terminology

Before we dive into measurement methods, it's important to understand key terms:

Diagonal Measurement

What it means: The distance from one corner of the screen to the diagonally opposite corner
Standard: Measured in inches (e.g., 24", 27", 32")
Why diagonal: Industry standard since CRT monitor days, makes comparison easier

Aspect Ratio

Common ratios: 16:9 (widescreen), 16:10, 21:9 (ultrawide), 32:9 (super ultrawide)
Impact: Two 24" monitors can have different dimensions if aspect ratios differ

Viewable Area vs. Total Size

Viewable area: The actual screen surface (what you measure)
Total size: Includes the bezel/frame (not part of screen size)

Physical Dimensions

Width × Height: Actual screen dimensions in inches or centimeters
Note: Varies based on aspect ratio even with same diagonal size

Method 1: Measure with a Ruler or Tape Measure (Most Accurate)

This is the most reliable method for determining your exact monitor size.

What You'll Need

  • Ruler, tape measure, or yardstick (at least 30 inches/75 cm long)
  • Good lighting
  • Optional: Second person to help with larger monitors

Step-by-Step Instructions

Step 1: Turn Off Your Monitor (Recommended)

Turning off the screen makes it easier to see the screen edges and avoid eyestrain from bright pixels.

Step 2: Identify the Screen Edges

Critical: Locate where the actual screen ends and the bezel (frame) begins.

Tips to identify screen edges:

  • Look for the slight border where the active display area meets the frame
  • On powered-off screens, you can often see a subtle color difference
  • Run your finger gently along the edge to feel where the glass/screen surface begins

Step 3: Position Your Measuring Tool

For rigid rulers:

  1. Place one end at the bottom-left corner of the screen (not the bezel)
  2. Extend diagonally to the top-right corner of the screen
  3. Ensure the ruler crosses the exact diagonal line

For tape measures:

  1. Hook or hold the tape end at one corner of the screen
  2. Pull the tape diagonally across to the opposite corner
  3. Keep the tape taut and straight

Step 4: Read the Measurement

Important tips:

  • Measure to the nearest quarter inch (0.25") or millimeter
  • Don't include the bezel/frame in your measurement
  • Round to the nearest standard monitor size (21", 22", 24", 27", etc.)

Step 5: Verify Your Measurement

Double-check by:

  • Measuring the opposite diagonal (bottom-right to top-left)
  • Comparing to manufacturer specifications (if available)
  • Ensuring your measurement makes sense (most monitors are 19"-49")

Common Measurement Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Including the bezel: Only measure the active screen area
❌ Measuring width or height: Monitor size is always diagonal
❌ Rounding too much: 23.8" should be called 24", but 23.5" might be marketed as 23.6"
❌ Not measuring straight: Keep your ruler perfectly diagonal

Measurement Accuracy Tips

For curved monitors:

  • Measure the straight-line diagonal distance, not following the curve
  • The curve doesn't affect the screen size rating

For ultrawide monitors:

  • Still measure diagonally, but expect larger numbers (34", 38", 49")
  • Don't measure width only

For multi-monitor setups:

  • Measure each monitor separately
  • Note the combined width if planning a replacement

Method 2: Check Your Computer's Display Settings (Fastest)

This method doesn't give you the physical screen size, but it provides your resolution and can help identify your monitor model.

For Windows 10/11

Quick method:

  1. Right-click on your desktop
  2. Select "Display settings"
  3. Scroll to "Display resolution"
  4. Note your current resolution (e.g., 1920×1080, 2560×1440)

Alternative method:

  1. Press Windows + I to open Settings
  2. Go to System → Display
  3. Click "Advanced display settings"
  4. View monitor name and resolution

What this tells you:

  • Your current resolution (not physical size)
  • Monitor model name (sometimes includes size in the name)
  • Refresh rate information

For macOS

Method:

  1. Click the Apple menu (top-left)
  2. Select "About This Mac"
  3. Click "Displays"
  4. View display information

Alternative:

  1. Go to System Preferences → Displays
  2. Hold Option key and click "Scaled"
  3. View detailed display information

For Linux

Using terminal:

xrandr | grep '*'

This shows your current resolution and connected displays.

Using system settings:

  • Navigate to Settings → Displays
  • View resolution and monitor information

Method 3: Check the Monitor Specifications (Most Reliable)

If you want to know the exact manufacturer-specified size, check the monitor's specifications.

Physical Stickers and Labels

Where to look:

  1. Back of the monitor: Most common location
  2. Bottom bezel: Sometimes printed on the front frame
  3. Stand or base: Specification label might be here

What to look for:

  • Model number (usually includes screen size, e.g., "U2723DE" = 27")
  • Direct size specification (e.g., "24-inch monitor")
  • Serial number area often lists size

Decode Model Numbers

Many manufacturers include screen size in model numbers:

Dell:

  • U2723DE = 27 inches
  • P2415Q = 24 inches

ASUS:

  • VG279Q = 27 inches
  • PA328Q = 32 inches

LG:

  • 27GL850 = 27 inches
  • 34WN80C = 34 inches

Samsung:

  • C27G55T = 27 inches
  • Odyssey G5 32 = 32 inches

BenQ:

  • SW270C = 27 inches
  • PD3200U = 32 inches

General pattern: Look for a 2-digit number near the beginning or middle of the model number.

Understanding Aspect Ratios and Dimensions

Screen size alone doesn't tell the full story. Two monitors with the same diagonal measurement can have different widths and heights.

How Aspect Ratio Affects Dimensions

Example: 27" monitors with different aspect ratios

16:9 (Standard widescreen):

  • Width: 23.5" (59.7 cm)
  • Height: 13.2" (33.6 cm)
  • Most common for gaming and general use

16:10 (Slightly taller):

  • Width: 22.7" (57.8 cm)
  • Height: 14.2" (36.1 cm)
  • Popular for productivity

21:9 (34" ultrawide, similar height to 27" 16:9):

  • Width: 31.4" (79.8 cm)
  • Height: 13.1" (33.2 cm)
  • Extra horizontal space

Calculating Width and Height from Diagonal

If you know diagonal and aspect ratio, you can calculate dimensions:

For 16:9 screens:

  • Width = Diagonal × 0.872
  • Height = Diagonal × 0.490

Example: 27" monitor (16:9)

  • Width = 27 × 0.872 = 23.5"
  • Height = 27 × 0.490 = 13.2"

Troubleshooting Common Issues

My Measurement Doesn't Match a Standard Size

Possible reasons:

  1. Unusual aspect ratio: Not 16:9 (try 16:10, 21:9)
  2. Older monitor: Pre-HD standard sizes (4:3 ratio)
  3. Measurement error: Try measuring again carefully
  4. Custom/professional monitor: Not consumer-standard size

Solution:

  • Search your model number online for exact specifications
  • Measure carefully excluding the bezel
  • Check if your monitor is ultrawide or has an unusual aspect ratio

Model Number Doesn't Include Screen Size

Some manufacturers don't follow the size-in-model-number convention.

Alternative methods:

  1. Search the full model number online
  2. Check the physical label on the monitor back
  3. Look up the model on the manufacturer's support page
  4. Use the manual (if available) or PDF manual download

My Monitor Shows as "Generic PnP Monitor"

This is common in Windows when drivers aren't installed.

To find your actual model:

  1. Open Device Manager
  2. Expand Monitors
  3. Right-click on Generic PnP Monitor
  4. Select Properties → Details tab
  5. Look for Hardware IDs which might contain the model

Or: Check the physical label on your monitor.

Online Tools Show Wrong Screen Size

Why this happens:

  • Screen size ≠ Resolution
  • Many tools show resolution, not physical size
  • Browser zoom settings affect online rulers

Solution: Use physical measurement or check manufacturer specifications.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I measure the screen or the bezel?

Only measure the screen, not the bezel (frame). The advertised monitor size refers only to the visible display area, excluding the plastic or metal frame around it.

Is monitor size measured diagonally?

Yes, always diagonally, from one corner of the screen to the diagonally opposite corner. This is the industry standard for all monitors and TVs.

How do I measure a curved monitor?

Measure the straight-line diagonal distance, not following the curve. Use a ruler or tape measure and measure as if the screen were flat, from corner to corner in a straight line.

What if my measurement is between standard sizes?

Round to the nearest standard size. For example:

  • 23.5"-23.9" = 24" monitor
  • 27.5"-28" = 27" monitor
  • 31.5"-32.5" = 32" monitor

Manufacturers sometimes specify 23.8" or 27.5", but these are marketed as 24" and 27" respectively.

Can I use my phone to measure monitor size?

Yes, if you have a measuring app that uses your phone's camera and AR capabilities. Apps like "Measure" (iOS) or "Measure" (Android) can estimate dimensions. However, physical measurement is more accurate.

How do I measure an ultrawide monitor?

Same method: Measure diagonally from corner to corner. Ultrawide monitors have larger diagonal measurements (34", 38", 49") but the measurement technique is identical.

What's the difference between monitor size and screen resolution?

Monitor size: Physical diagonal measurement (e.g., 27")
Screen resolution: Number of pixels (e.g., 2560×1440)

They're independent:

  • Same size can have different resolutions
  • Same resolution can appear on different sizes

How accurate does my measurement need to be?

Within ±0.5 inch is sufficient for most purposes. Manufacturers themselves have small variations (a "27 inch" might be 27.0" or 27.5"). Round to the nearest standard size for practical purposes.

Best Practices and Pro Tips

For Buying a New Monitor

  1. Measure your current monitor if you're replacing it
  2. Measure your desk space to ensure fit
  3. Calculate optimal viewing distance:
    • 1080p: 1.5× to 2× screen diagonal
    • 1440p: 1× to 1.5× screen diagonal
    • 4K: 0.7× to 1× screen diagonal

For Multi-Monitor Setups

  1. Measure each monitor individually
  2. Calculate total width including bezels
  3. Consider height alignment (some monitors adjustable)
  4. Plan for cable management space behind monitors

For Monitor Arms and Mounts

  1. Check VESA pattern (not just size)
  2. Note monitor weight (critical for arm capacity)
  3. Measure depth with stand removed
  4. Consider tilt and rotation clearance
